Food

Discover the Foods that Keep You Feeling Great

Eating a healthy diet is crucial for maintaining good health and preventing chronic diseases. But what is healthy food, exactly? In this page, we will explore what healthy food is, give examples of healthy foods, and provide tips on how to incorporate them into your diet. We will also discuss what to avoid if you want to eat a healthy diet.

Fruit and Vegetables

Most of us still are not eating enough fruit and vegetables. They should make up just over a third of the food we eat each day, at least 5 portions of a variety of fruit and veg each day.

Some examples of fruts and vegetables are:

  • Apples
  • Oranges
  • Carrots
  • Bannanas
  • Advocado

Remember that fruit juice and smoothies should be limited to no more than a combined total of 150ml a day.

Fruit and vegetables are a good source of vitamins, minerals and fibre.

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are one of the main sources of energy for the body. They are found in foods such as grains, fruits, vegetables, and dairy products.

Some examples of healthy carbohydrates include:

  • Whole grain breads and cereals
  • Brown rice
  • Quinoa
  • Sweet potatoes
  • Beans and legumes

It's important to choose complex carbohydrates, which are high in fiber and provide a steady release of energy, over simple carbohydrates that can cause spikes in blood sugar levels.

Carbohydrates should make up about 45-65% of your daily calorie intake, but the specific amount may vary depending on your individual needs and activity level.

Proteins

Proteins are essential nutrients that are important for growth and repair of the body's tissues. They are found in a variety of foods, including:

  • Meat, poultry, and fish
  • Eggs
  • Beans and legumes
  • Nuts and seeds
  • Dairy products

Proteins are made up of amino acids, which are the building blocks of the body. Some amino acids can be produced by the body, but others must be obtained through the diet. These are called essential amino acids.

The amount of protein you need each day depends on factors such as your age, gender, weight, and activity level. In general, adults should aim for a minimum of 0.8 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day.

It's important to choose lean sources of protein and to vary your protein sources to ensure you get all of the essential amino acids your body needs.

Dairy

Dairy products are a good source of calcium, which is important for building and maintaining strong bones and teeth. They also provide other important nutrients, such as vitamin D and protein.

Some examples of dairy products include:

  • Milk
  • Cheese
  • Yogurt
  • Butter
  • Sour cream

It's important to choose low-fat or fat-free dairy products to limit your intake of saturated fats. If you are lactose intolerant or have a dairy allergy, there are non-dairy alternatives available that are fortified with calcium and vitamin D.

Adults should aim for 2-3 servings of dairy products per day, but the specific amount may vary depending on your individual needs and dietary restrictions.

Fats

Fats are an essential part of a healthy diet, but it's important to choose the right types of fats and to consume them in moderation.

There are two kinds of fats, Saturated fats are mainly found in animal-based foods, while unsaturated fats are mostly found in plant-based foods.

Saturated fats have been linked to negative health outcomes, while unsaturated fats are associated with positive health outcomes, including a reduced risk of heart disease, lower cholesterol levels, and improved brain function.

Some foods that include unsaturated fats include:

  • Avocado
  • Nuts and seeds
  • Fatty fish, such as salmon and tuna
  • Olive oil
  • Coconut oil

Some foods that contain saturated fats include:

  • Fatty cuts of meat, such as beef and pork
  • Butter and lard
  • Full-fat dairy products, such as whole milk, cheese, and cream
  • Fried foods, such as french fries and fried chicken
  • Baked goods, such as cakes, cookies, and pastries, that are made with butter or partially hydrogenated oils
  • Processed snacks, such as potato chips and crackers, that are made with partially hydrogenated oils or other sources of trans fats

Saturated and trans fats should be limited in your diet, as they can increase your risk of heart disease.

Fats should make up about 20-35% of your daily calorie intake, but the specific amount may vary depending on your individual needs and dietary restrictions.

Remember to also pay attention to portion sizes and to balance your intake of fats with other important nutrients, such as carbohydrates, proteins, and fruits and vegetables.
